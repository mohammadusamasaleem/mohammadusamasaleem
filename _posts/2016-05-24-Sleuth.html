It uses kallisto's bootstrap estimates to implement novel statistical methods for differential analysis. background: > RNA-Sequencing, or RNA-seq for short, was developed in 2008 and is a type of study that aims, at a high level, to understand gene expression within an organism. RNA-seq is used to analyze the transcriptome, which, unlike the genome, is constantly changing. Among other things, RNA-seq helps us understand what types of conditions can cause cells to express certain genes (differential analysis), and helps us discover new transcripts and explore their purpose.

In an RNA-seq experiment, RNA from an organism is chemically prepared and fed through a sequencer. The sequencer generates "reads" in the millions: each read is a chunk of RNA-molecule that came from the original organism. A computer program, such as kallisto, is then used to find which genes these reads came from. This process is called loosely called "alignment." The more reads that mapped to a particular gene, the more that gene was being expressed at the time of the sequencing. Due to the sheer volume of data, there are many difficult computational problems involved with RNA-seq alignment, many of which are discussed in the kallisto paper.

After the reads have been aligned, another computer program, such as sleuth, is used to determine, statistically speaking, which genes were most differentially expressed across a condition. Whether or not a gene was significantly different across a condition is typically determined by p-value or q-value. From these analyses, computational biologists draw conclusions about which genes are important to which conditions; an example is Sharma et al., a paper discussing genes that are important when cells die.

The above Sharma et al. paper is particularly important to me because it was the first RNA-seq paper I ever read! my_role: > My role in this project was twofold. First, I designed several of the data visualizations present in the Sleuth live app (see the example anlysis). I designed the gene table and the gene viewer, which display the variation across conditions of given genes. I created the transcript heat map plot, which uses a hierarchical clustering algorithm to group transcripts with similar expression patterns together. Clustered heat maps are useful to chunk large data sets into related groups. I implemented the volcano plot, a classic plot in statistics with significance of expression on the y-axis and fold-change on the x-axis. This plot is especially useful to find outlying transcripts that have high fold-change but low significance. My implementation of the plot is reactive, and lets a user highlight particular data points and analyze those points in a stand-alone table.

Second, I made it possible for high-volume Sleuth objects to be shared online. Initially, the Sleuth objects and anlyses were too large to be shared online because they stored statistical bootstraps of the original data to generate the data visualizations live in the browser. I refactored the Sleuth code to pre-compute key statistcs needed to display the plots. This allowed Sleuth to abandon the cumbersome bootstrap data after pre-computation. This effectively reduced the size of the analyses objects by orders of magnitude, for the first time allowing RNA-seq analyses to shared live in a browser. Before Sleuth, RNA-seq experiments were only available as raw data in the Short Read Archive and needed several days and multiple cores to re-analyze.

I am now working on compiling a database of re-analyzed RNA-seq experiments that will shortly be a live webpage. For now, check out the Github repo for the database here. --- Sleuth is an RNA-seq tool that performs differential expression analysis on quantified reads from kallisto. Sleuth is novel both in its use of statistical bootstraps to understand differential gene expression, and in its use of Shiny to provide in-browser, interactive analyses of RNA-seq experiments. If you are interested in using sleuth, see the sleuth homepage. If you would like to see an example of a live sleuth analysis, see the bear's lair.
