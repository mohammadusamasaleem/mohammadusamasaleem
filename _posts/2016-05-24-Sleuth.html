It was developed by the Pachter Lab at UC Berkeley. background: > RNA-Sequencing, or RNA-seq for short, was developed in 2008 and is a type of study that aims, at a high level, to understand gene expression within an organism. RNA-seq is used to analyze the transcriptome, which, unlike the genome, is constantly changing. Among other things, RNA-seq helps us understand what types of conditions can cause cells to express certain genes (differential analysis), and helps us discover new transcripts and explore their purpose.

In an RNA-seq experiment, RNA from an organism is chemically prepared and fed through a sequencer. The sequencer generates "reads" in the millions: each read is a chunk of RNA-molecule that came from the original organism. A computer program, such as Kallisto, is then used to find which genes these reads came from. This process is called loosely called "alignment." The more reads that mapped to a particular gene, the more that gene was being expressed at the time of the sequencing. Due to the sheer volume of data, there are many difficult computational problems involved with RNA-seq alignment, many of which are discussed in the Kallisto Paper.

After the reads have been aligned, another computer program, such as Sleuth, is used to determine, statistically speaking, which genes were most differentially expressed across a condition. Whether or not a gene was significantly different across a condition is typically determined by p-value or q-value. my_role: > --- Sleuth is an RNA-seq tool that analyzes quantified reads from Kallisto. Sleuth is novel both in its use of statistical bootstraps to understand differential gene expression, and in its use of Shiny to provide in-browser, interactive analyses of RNA-seq experiments. It was developed by the Pachter Lab at UC Berkeley. More about Sleuth can be found at the Sleuth homepage.
